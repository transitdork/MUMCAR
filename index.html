What to watch for, in plain English:

MG's 10-year warranty has a catch

MG advertises up to 10 years/250,000km, but you only get past the standard 7 years if every service is done at an MG dealer, on schedule. Skip one, or use an independent mechanic even once, and you drop back to 7 years for the rest of ownership. If your Mum wants the longer warranty, she needs to commit to MG's own service centres the whole time.

Hyundai's 7-year warranty has the same catch

As of September 2025, new Hyundais (including Kona Electric) get a 7-year unlimited-km warranty — but only if every scheduled service happens at a Hyundai dealer, on time. Otherwise it's the older 5-year standard. This is a very similar condition to MG's, just starting from a shorter base.

Renault is the only capped-kilometre warranty here

Every other car in this comparison has an unlimited-kilometre warranty (or close to it). The Megane E-Tech's is capped at 100,000 km over 5 years — for a low-mileage driver like your Mum (around 7,500 km/year) that's not a practical concern, since she'd take over 13 years to reach it, but it's worth knowing this is the one warranty here with a genuine mileage ceiling.

BYD's warranty recently got simpler and better

Under BYD's original distributor (EVDirect), the Atto 3's warranty had a confusing patchwork of shorter periods on individual components — lights and suspension covered for only 4 years, infotainment and the charging port for 3 years, the 12V battery for just 1 year. In April 2025, BYD announced a simplified warranty structure that removed these shorter carve-outs, and backdated the improvement to cover every Atto 3 sold since the model launched in 2022. This is a genuine improvement worth knowing about if anyone mentions the old, more complicated terms.

Leapmotor & Zeekr: straightforward on paper, but young brands

Both list clean, unconditional warranty terms (6yr/150,000km and 5yr unlimited respectively) with no unusual catches found in research. The caveat isn't the paperwork — it's that both are new enough to the Australian market that there's no long track record yet of how smoothly warranty claims actually get handled here in practice.

Volvo EX40's servicing is genuinely free, not just capped

Every other car in this comparison offers capped-price servicing — you still pay for each visit, just at a pre-agreed amount. The EX40 is the only one here where Volvo includes 5 years/150,000km of servicing at no cost at all, plus 5 years of roadside assistance, bundled into the purchase price. Its warranty itself is a standard 5-year unlimited-km term with no unusual conditions — the free servicing is the real standout, and it's a genuine point in the EX40's favour against its otherwise steep price.

General warranty tips:
• "Whichever comes first" means both the years AND the kilometre limit apply — hitting either one ends that part of the warranty, even if the other hasn't been reached.
• Battery warranties typically guarantee the battery won't degrade below 70–75% of its original capacity within the warranty period — not that it won't degrade at all. Normal, gradual capacity loss is expected and isn't itself a claimable fault.
• Commercial/rideshare use usually shortens warranties across the board (often to 5 years/150,000km) — not relevant for personal use, but worth knowing if the car is ever used commercially.
• Warranties are almost always transferable to a subsequent owner if resold within the period, which helps resale value.
• Always ask the dealer to confirm current terms in writing at the time of purchase — warranty terms do change (as BYD's did in 2025), and dealer paperwork is what actually governs a claim, not a webpage.
